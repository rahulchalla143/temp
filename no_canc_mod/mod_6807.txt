CHIEF COMPLAINT:  Rule out cholangitis, evaluation for cholecystectomy.
HISTORY OF PRESENT ILLNESS:  Patient was a 68-year-old male Cantonese speaking who presented with 2 day history of right upper quadrant pain with associated chills and fever, positive jaundice, no emesis, no nausea.
Pain onset was shortly after eating fatty meal the prior evening.
The patient presented with some pain, but slightly improved.
Patient with known periampullary diverticulum.
PAST MEDICAL HISTORY:  As noted above, hepatitis B, hypertension, diabetes and increased cholesterol.
ALLERGIES:  No known drug allergies.
No stigmata of chronic liver disease was noted.
No palmar erythema.
Recommendations included proceeding with cholecystectomy as there was no clinical or radiological evidence of cirrhosis and liver biopsy was recommended intraop.
No apparent complications were noted.
Patient was a bit tachy postoperatively.
Patient experienced minimal pain.
Abdomen appeared minimally distended and nontender.
He was ambulatory and safe for discharge.
DISCHARGE DIAGNOSES:  Cholangitis, choledochocholelithiasis, hypertension and diabetes.
