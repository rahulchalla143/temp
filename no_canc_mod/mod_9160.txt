HISTORY OF PRESENT ILLNESS:  This is a 44-year-old male with cardiac risk factors which include history of smoking, family history, hyperlipidemia, who complains of nausea and vomiting that started the night prior to presentation at 7 pm.
The patient denies sick contacts or suspicious foods.
He reports sweats and chills which persisted throughout the night and also reported the onset of substernal chest pain as well, which was nonradiating, felt like pressure "stiff" with tingling down the left upper extremity as well.
The patient tried to alleviate symptoms with drinking water, but only kept vomiting.
The patient continued to have chest pain until he arrived there at which point, he was started on a nitroglycerin drip.
The patient reports no PND, no orthopnea, no pedal edema, and no claudication.
Gastroesophageal reflux disease.
Family history of cardiac disease.
HEENT:  No icterus, no pallor.
Cardiovascular: Regular, rate, and rhythm, no murmurs, rubs, or gallops, no jugular venous distention, no peripheral edema.
No focal neurological deficits.
Extremities:  Right groin catheter site, no bruit, no drainage.
HOSPITAL COURSE:  Patient was diagnosed with inferior wall myocardial infarction and was taken immediately to the catheterization laboratory where his right circumflex artery was stented.
An electrocardiogram at the time of presentation apparently showed Q waves in II, III, aVF, V5 and V6, ST depressions in I and L, ST elevations in II, III, aVF, V1 through V3, T-wave inversions in II, III, aVF, and V6.
The patient did not report any recurrent chest pain and did well with Physical Therapy.
There were no complications with his groin site.
DISCHARGE DIAGNOSIS:  Inferior wall myocardial infarction.
